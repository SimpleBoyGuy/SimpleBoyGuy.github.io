APDPO for FJSP

Reinforcement Learning for the Flexible Job Shop Scheduling Problem

This project studies how reinforcement learning can be used to solve the Flexible Job Shop Scheduling Problem (FJSP), with the goal of producing scheduling policies that are both high-quality and practical for complex manufacturing decision-making.

Project Overview

The Flexible Job Shop Scheduling Problem extends classical job shop scheduling by allowing each operation to be processed on multiple candidate machines. This flexibility makes the problem much more realistic for practical production systems, but also significantly increases the complexity of making high-quality scheduling decisions.

In this project, I explore reinforcement learning as a way to learn scheduling policies directly from problem instances and system feedback, instead of relying only on handcrafted dispatching rules or expensive iterative optimization procedures.

This page is based directly on my CDC 2025 paper Action-Priority Driven Policy Optimization for Flexible Job Shop Scheduling Problem.

Problem Description

In FJSP, each job consists of an ordered sequence of operations with precedence constraints. Every operation can be processed by one machine chosen from its eligible machine set, and different machines may require different processing times.

A solver therefore needs to decide both which operation should be processed next and which machine should handle it. Because sequencing and machine assignment are tightly coupled, the search space grows rapidly as the system size increases.

The optimization objective in the paper is to minimize the makespan, namely the final completion time of the schedule.

MDP Formulation

The paper formulates FJSP as a finite-horizon Markov Decision Process. Each stage corresponds to a decision point where at least one feasible machine-operation assignment is available.

  • State: job progress matrix, machine availability vector, active work set, and system clock
  • Action: assigning one machine to one feasible operation
  • Dynamics: update job status, machine status, work set, and advance the system clock when necessary
  • Reward: negative elapsed time between consecutive decision stages

Under this design, the total return over a trajectory becomes the negative makespan, so maximizing return is equivalent to minimizing the scheduling objective.

Why Reinforcement Learning

Traditional approaches to FJSP often involve a trade-off. Exact optimization methods can provide strong solutions but may be computationally expensive, while heuristic and metaheuristic methods can be faster but may sacrifice consistency or optimality.

Reinforcement learning offers a different perspective by learning a sequential decision policy directly from scheduling states. For me, the appeal is not only better performance, but also the possibility of building scheduling solvers that are adaptive, efficient, and practically deployable.

APDPO

My CDC 2025 work introduces Action-Priority Driven Policy Optimization (APDPO), a reinforcement learning method designed specifically for FJSP. The method is motivated by three main goals: improving solution quality, increasing interpretability through a more intuitive policy representation, and reducing computational cost through direct policy updates.

  • Action-priority policy representation for more transparent scheduling decisions
  • Direct, gradient-free policy updates for improved computational efficiency
  • A reinforcement learning framework tailored to the structural characteristics of FJSP

Policy Representation

Instead of representing the policy only through a neural network, APDPO uses an action-priority vector. Each entry corresponds to one possible action, and each value indicates the priority of that action.

For a given state, the policy is obtained by normalizing the priority values over the feasible action set. During training, this yields a stochastic policy; during inference, the deterministic policy simply selects the feasible action with the highest priority.

Policy Update Mechanism

A central feature of APDPO is that the policy update is gradient-free. When a selected action is associated with improvement, the algorithm directly increases its priority and proportionally reduces the priorities of competing actions. This leads to an update rule that is simple, direct, and easy to interpret.

The paper further proves policy-improvement results under explicit conditions. In other words, APDPO is not only an empirically effective algorithm, but also a method supported by theoretical analysis.

Interpretation

One of the most interesting interpretations in the paper is that updating the action-priority vector can be viewed as implicitly refining the problem instance itself. Since priorities are initialized from processing-time information, changing priorities can be understood as modifying the effective structure seen by the policy, so that a simple deterministic scheduler performs better on the refined instance.

Experimental Results

The paper evaluates APDPO on the standard Brandimarte and Hurink benchmark datasets for FJSP. According to the reported results, APDPO delivers stronger solution quality than existing RL baselines and moves significantly closer to OR-Tools, while also showing lower inference time.

For me, this is especially important because it suggests that reinforcement learning for scheduling can be both academically strong and computationally practical.

Current Focus

The broader project aims to develop a reinforcement learning solver for scheduling problems that is not only academically strong, but also meaningful for real-world applications. I am especially interested in connecting rigorous algorithmic ideas with practical scheduling environments where decision quality, efficiency, and scalability all matter.
